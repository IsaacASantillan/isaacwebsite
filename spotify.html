data ethics AI Is Using Data for Things We Are Unaware Of
hot take #1

One of my favorite classes at UC Berkeley was Data Ethics. One of the more interesting conversations we had was a discussion on data collection. In 2017, Amazon bought Whole Foods. Since then, almost every Whole Foods collects data from its shoppers on items they buy, when they buy them, and how often — in order to suggest item recommendations next time they are in the Amazon shopping cart.

Although this seems harmless, what does this data actually tell us? If you are buying multi-grain bread every Tuesday instead of the discounted white bread, you likely make more money. If AI were to take over this data and had to decide which customer to save in a life or death situation, who would it choose? This is just one example of how seemingly harmless data can have larger consequences in the future. Now imagine what can be done with even more personal data.

career Computer Scientists Are Not Just Programmers
hot take #2

Computer Scientists do not just code. We have "scientist" in our name for a reason. I hate this idea that we can only apply for software engineering jobs — we are much more than that. We learn about data ethics, surveillance, and artificial intelligence.

If you are an agency, you should be fighting for computer scientists. I hate how jobs exclude computer scientists from eligible majors. Computer scientists are needed across public transportation, politics, and education more than ever as AI continues to expand. Coding is simply one of many things we can do.

transit Public Transportation Can Work
hot take #3

Ever since joining BART, I've been intrigued by why public transportation in the US failed. After watching the documentary "Taken For a Ride," it is clear that General Motors may have single-handedly destroyed public transportation for everyone.

It is diabolical what they did. They essentially took out the trolley systems in major cities like Boston, LA, and Philadelphia and replaced them with buses — destroying the infrastructure for those trolley systems. Without infrastructure in place, it was nearly impossible to convince voters to build new tracks. Then they reduced bus frequency. Absolutely diabolical. Public transit can still work — it just needs the chance.
